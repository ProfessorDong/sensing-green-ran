To probe robustness when the ISAC posterior itself is wrong, we ran a model-mismatch sweep on Mon Milan plus Geolife at $V{=}10^4$, $\Gamma{=}150$, $\beta{=}0.95$ over 10 canonical seeds, varying a constant per-UE position bias $b\in\{0,2,5,10\}$ m injected into $\hat{x}$ (Sweep A) and a covariance-underestimate factor $\sigma_{\rm true}/\sigma_{\rm rep}\in\{1,2,4,8\}$ that shrinks the controller-observed $\hat\Sigma$ (Sweep B). Results are summarized in Figure~\ref{fig:mismatch} and Table~\ref{tab:mismatch}. Under position bias the picture is mixed but instructive, with the two controllers indistinguishable up to $b=5$ m and DR-CVaR overtaking Sens-Lyap on tail-CVaR only at $b=10$ m, where DR drops the empirical CVaR from $631.3$ to $575.4$ (an $8.9\%$ tail reduction) at a $5.5\%$ power premium; the exceedance rate $\Pr(\ell>\Gamma)$ remains slightly higher for DR ($0.147$ vs $0.114$) because the virtual-queue charge still triggers conservative wake-ups even when no excursion ultimately occurs. Under covariance underestimate both controllers degrade together and the DR margin collapses, with CVaR essentially tied at factor $4\times$ and $8\times$ ($659$ vs $653$ and $665$ vs $651$, respectively); this is consistent with Theorem~3 because the regret bound scales linearly with $\varepsilon(\hat\Sigma)=\kappa\sqrt{{\rm tr}(\hat\Sigma)}+\kappa_0$, so an over-confident filter starves the DR term of its safety budget. The takeaway is that the DR-CVaR layer pays off precisely in the regime it was designed for, namely bounded mean-shift in the ISAC posterior, while a separate calibration check on $\hat\Sigma$ (or a larger $\kappa_0$) is needed to keep tail reliability when the sensor mis-reports its own confidence.
